Anticoagulant Drug Interactions — Warfarin and NSAIDs

Concurrent administration of warfarin (Coumadin) and non-steroidal
anti-inflammatory drugs (NSAIDs) such as ibuprofen, naproxen, or aspirin
significantly increases the risk of major bleeding, particularly upper
gastrointestinal hemorrhage. The mechanism is dual: NSAIDs inhibit platelet
aggregation by blocking COX-1, and they also displace warfarin from plasma
albumin binding sites, increasing the free fraction of the anticoagulant.

Clinical recommendation: avoid the combination when possible. If concurrent
use is medically necessary, prescribe the lowest effective NSAID dose for
the shortest duration, monitor INR weekly for the first month, and consider
prophylactic proton pump inhibitor (PPI) therapy. Patients should be
counseled to report any signs of bleeding immediately: black or tarry
stools, hematuria, prolonged bleeding from cuts, or unexplained bruising.

Acetaminophen (paracetamol) up to 2 grams per day is generally preferred
for analgesia in patients on chronic warfarin therapy. Doses above 3 grams
per day for more than one week may potentiate warfarin and elevate INR.
